Installation, Cinnamon, spilling Wine

I have been the unproud owner of a Windows 11 laptop for about 2.5ish years now. I regularly have stated how much of a bastard child this poor laptop is to me, and every step of the way in using it has had me sticking my tongue out and crossing my arms like a child being subjected to vegetables. Although that hatred has been increasingly more justly founded, with even non-tech-savvy users complaining of the recent AI practices Microsoft has been pushing, the security nightmare of co-pilot, bloat clogging system resources, and of course the infamous update system.

I originally researched Linux sometime last year at the recommendation of some friends, but finally took the plunge May of this year. My two choices were between Mint and Ubuntu, and I settled on Mint since it seemed like a more natural selection for me. I spent about 2 days backing up my files on my storage USB, and an extra day waiting for my webring e-mail password to reset itself. (This is the second time I've forgotten my webring e-mail but I swear its backed up now and has a better recovery method) I used balenaEtcher to format a spare USB I had and went into my BIOS to get started. Heres a fun fact about me no one really needed to know, but the only computer OS I've ever touched in my life has been Windows! I've never even seen a Mac in the metal.

Installation went incredibly smooth and I was already having a blast sniffing around the system settings. I was calling Benjamin while first playing with it and I remarked how happy I was its default browser is Firefox! I was expecting it to have its own weird native web browser or using the terminal to install the one you wanted. I looked at some default applications it had like Rhythmbox, Hexchat, and Thunderbird. On my first install I uninstalled Hexchat, but couldn't get rid of Thunderbird for some reason with its dependencies.

I was having alot of fun my first night of using it but made my first grave error. For whatever reason, I thought it'd be a great idea to try and install Wine at the odd hour of 3 AM. I needed Wine in order to get my beloved Toontown Corporate Clash running (more on that later, turns out I didn't) I have no idea what button I pressed or even what error I really got but I knew for certain I fumbled the bag somewhere along the way. Maybe it was trying to install winetricks before running winecfg, maybe it was doing every command as sudo, the world may never know. I ended up doing a system restore the next day using Timeshift. That seemed to largely fix it although a few folders remained in my home directory. Not that it mattered much anyway for whats next.

Swapping to XFCE and General Notes

Some may recall a ancient blog post from a time long pass of when I wrote about using Windows Blinds from Stardocks to customize my systems. I later uninstalled it after someone I was friends with (Keyword, *was*) informed me Windows Updates broke it. But my quench for desktop customization never was fulfilled.. And this was one of my main reasons for even swapping to Linux anyways. I began researching about customizing, or "ricing" as I learned its called. But for some reason, whenever I'd apply a theme it would look a bit.. off. I was confused since the numerous videos I had been watching seemed to be for Cinnamon, and then I noticed that one little window was missing from my options. "Window style". It seems that recent updates to Cinnamon got rid of this option; further research on the topic appears to confirm this.

I was rather stuck on where to go next, I learned a bit more about what desktop enviorments do, and came to the conclusion I should probably swap to XFCE. I saw some things about being able to have *two* desktop enviorments, but also heard it was unstable and definetely not something for beginner plebs like myself. I reformatted my USB for XFCE (Really cool that it has a built in feature to verify the ISO for you instead of having to download another program!) and reinstalled. I was a bit worried that something would go awry with reinstalling drivers a second time and wiping the system ;admittedly I don't know much about how drivers work right now so this might be entirely unfounded But all went well, and I have been using XFCE since then. It fits very comfortably for me. :]

Anyways, enough talk about installing the OS. How does it work in practice, and what're the differences between Windows? Immediately, you have alot of control over your inner system. It was really cool sorting around the main files of my system (And of course I was very careful not to trip over anything) and seeing system processes. You're gonna be alot more involved using your computer, and having to remember some basic terminal commands is required; and some decent troubleshooting skills for programs. (Totally salty here, but for example its not for the kinds of people who're on r/neocities asking how to center text) The upside is theres alot of guides and tutorials online for downloading software and programs and also for any potential error you'd face. I'd recommend checking out the Linux Mint Forums for the latest discussions, if you're not using Mint theres a forum for literally every one under the sun. I briefly peeked into the Ubuntu forums sometimes, since Mint is based off of it.

I had prior experience using the Windows CMD prompt, so getting used to the terminal wasn't a challenge for me. Using the terminal really isn't all that scary as you'd think! Its really not some elite hacker thing, but I'd recommend making a txt on your desktop of quick to remember commands. Some things I wanted to do required it, like Panda3D and yt-dlp. (We'll talk more in-depth about those later) But their setup was incredibly easy. I'd recommend taking it slow and reading up tutorials and posts about the thing you're trying to use the terminal for, (Like installing something) and get ready to learn all about what dependencies it needs.

Updates are very easy to install too and not invasive or demanding. (Or restart my fucking computer while I'm using it and re-enabling shit I turned off) You should notice in the bottom right of your taskbar a little shield icon, this is the "Update Manager". You can also run "sudo apt update" in the terminal, but I use this more. My only warning is to be careful about kernel updates! I saw a kernel update a while ago and had a odd feeling about it, and looking it up on the Linux Mint Forums it seems that everyone was having issues with it. So keep an eye out for if it wants to up your kernel version (This is different from just security updates to the one you already have installed!) but if you turn on your computer to a black screen, look up how to boot into the grub menu. I had to do this once on another computer.

Applications and Functions

Lets talk about some of the default programs Mint came with, and how often I use them. One of my favorite system functions has to be Timeshift, the backup program. I'd recommend making quite a few backups while you first start installing things and setting up your system. My amount of snapshots I have scheduled to take are about 2 weekly and 2 daily. (Although I've seen some say thats over-kill) I'd recommend making one before you install a new program, before you do a system update, and to keep atleast one from the start of the month. Make sure to go back in there to delete older ones! Not only for system space, but for more accurate shots too.

Thunderbird is a mail client of sorts, made by darling to all dorks, Mozilla. I did not feel like fighting with Protonmail to get my e-mails working, it looked a bit convoluted with installing some other program. But I have been using it as a RSS reader! Finally I have one!! Please make your site a RSS feed! I'm literally following myself since theres so little!

Theres also Rhythmbox, the mp3/audio media reader. I found out its also able to edit the metadata for audio files, which was very nice since I need it for my iPod. Theres also Libre Office, which is Linux's alternative to Microsoft Office from what I've heard. I piddled around in Writer for a bit when writing an application for something that required its format. Interesting little critter..

To install programs, theres a few things you can do. For one, you can use the "Software Manager" which is like a giant repository for different applications. (Although be careful, sometimes programs listed here may be outdated/worse then just downloading directly from something like the official Github.) You can also run terminal commands, use appimages, or .deb files. The programs I wanted to install used all of these methods, but it's nothing to sweat about. When in doubt about what to do with an extension you don't recognize, please God, look up some answers first!

Now onto programs I installed and use regularly. Discord has always been a total resource hog, so I now use the web client. Discord has a few.. odd issues on Linux. This is apparently a whole issue on Linux, and anything I looked up about it had mixed results pointing to negatives. I also noticed while using the software client was that it wouldn't tell me when I'd get a new message. The taskbar "(1)" didn't appear like when I used it on web client. Very cool Discord, very cool..

Next order of business was getting my favorite art program, FireAlpaca. I discovered that FireAlpaca has Linux support in the form of an appimage. I had one issue however, I could not get custom brushes to install from the shop. Anything I looked up heiled no results. FireAlpaca users are an endangered species as is, but a FireAlpaca-Linux user is probably a new breed of beetle. I decided to give Krita a try after hearing raving reviews about it. And I certainly see why! Its brush selection is impressive and I recall it having very unique functions. But then presented a new issue, was that I've been using FireAlpaca for almost a decade at this point. So getting used to an entirely new program, while not impossible, was incredibly disorienting. Determined, I returned to FireAlpaca to get to the bottom of why the brushes weren't working. Whenever I'd click "install" on a brush, it would just do nothing. So I started watching some videos about how FireAlpaca brushes work, and how appimages work; and realized it being an appimage was the problem. What I believe was happening was that FireAlpaca had no place to store the brushes. Defeated again by no answers in search engines, I looked around the top bar settings and saw something like "open config folder" or something like that. Clicking it opened a folder for FireAlpaca! Should you get into Linux?

My months long journey into using Linux has been a ride I've greatly enjoyed, its really made using computers feel fun again. The possibilites and amount of distros out there to tinker with really make it a never ending rabbithole of things to do. My only regret has really been not swapping sooner. ..But with that said, even as a pretensious nerd, and a new found member of the Linux cult, I have to say I don't think Linux is for everyone.

The bitter truth is not every person is tech savvy or has the time to be swapping their entire computer. Learning about which distro to use, finding a USB to flash, and then figuring out how to configure all their previous programs. And some people just don't simply have that computer brain in them to navigate it even with a tutorial. And trust me, I love making fun of normies and dense netizens as much as the next Arch user. But I don't think its entirely their fault the way computers, and the internet as a whole, is the way it is. This is a much more larger rant for another day in another blog entry, but my tldr is we should be more angry at these bigger companies like Microsoft for holding a monopoly on the computer market.

I don't know the answers to these big questions, so I can only speculate on what I've seen and heard. I don't think Linux is a miracle pill for all the issues in the computer world, (Like for example, its "immunity to viruses" is a flat out myth, and just that people don't make as many for them since no one really expects them.) but I think the main issue is a lack of options. I think many internet users today are uninformed about many things like their privacy and how their computer even really works. Look at the massive uptick of ransomware attacks within the past few years.

And my dark confession is that I still do occasionally use Windows, being my Windows 10 tower PC for larger games or just ones that don't have any way to run. And there we have another reason I just demonstrated myself, that theres just some things Linux can't run. Its a bit of a vicious cycle with "game doesn't have Linux support > no one feels encouraged to swap to Linux > game doesn't bother making support since no one uses Linux". But you might say, "But Beau, if theres a bunch of operating systems in the world, so many games would take eons optimizing for them!" I'm very aware, as you just saw me use this hypothetical person for, my point is things like this can't continue. And we atleast need choices.

With that said, I hate the doomerism of a "computer apocalypse" with Bill Gates getting laser eyes and sacrifising his employees. My hope is that people will protest these things more and for me, my personal freedom in a way, was swapping. So take that with what you will.
